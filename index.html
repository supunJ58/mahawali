Udawalawe Reservoir

The Udawalawe Reservoir, located in the Ratnapura and Monaragala districts of Sri Lanka, is a critical reservoir constructed under the Mahaweli Irrigation Project in 1969. It contributes to the supply of water for irrigation, hydroelectricity generation and domestic consumption. The reservoir plays a vital role in supporting dry zone agriculture, particularly paddy cultivation, in the southern region. Its catchment area supports the Udawalawe National Park and is also a hub for tourism, enhancing biodiversity..

Chandrika Lake

The Chandrika Wewa Reservoir in Embilipitiya, located in the Ratnapura District near the Hambantota border, is one of the largest reservoirs built in Sri Lanka after gaining independence. The Chandrika Wewa project marked the first phase of a broader plan to irrigate a large portion of the Walawe Ganga river basin, initially targeting 5,000 acres of farmland. It was followed by the Udawalawe Project, which aimed to bring 50,000 acres under cultivation. The third phase of the initiative was the Samanala Wewa Project, situated closer to the river‚Äôs source, and it was designed to irrigate an additional 40,000 acres (Amunugama, 1965)..

Samanala Wewa

The Samanalawewa project gained significant attention due to water leakage problems encountered during its initial trial impoundment (Laksiri, 2007; Laksiri et al., 2005). In October 1992 a flat water table was observed up to a distance of 2.5 km on the right bank of the dam and also, a landslip occurred approximately 300 m downstream of the dam generating serious concern amongst the local residents that the dam was failing and there would be a serious loss of life (Khaniya et al., 2020; Laksiri et al., 2005; Udayakumara & Gunawardena, 2018). As a result of these incidents, it was decided to reduce the water level of the reservoir to 430 m and this level is maintained until the present time (Laksiri, 2007; Udayakumara & Gunawardena, 2018). Several remedial measures such as grout curtain and wet blanketing were undertaken to seal off the leakage but all the attempts failed (Laksiri et al., 2009; Udayakumara & Gunawardena, 2018). Therefore, the reservoir is still operating with a continuous water leakage of 1,800 litres per second (Laksiri, 2007; Laksiri et al., 2005).

Mahagama Tank/Urusita Wewa

Urusita Wewa (or Urusawa Tank) is a significant ancient Sri Lankan reservoir, famous for its remarkably preserved, unique stone-carved seven-hooded cobra guardstone (Naga sculpture) and the ancient Bisokotuwa (valve-pit), showcasing sophisticated ancient hydraulic engineering, dating back to the Anuradhapura period (around the 3rd Century BC) in the Moneragala District. This site highlights the advanced water management and cultural beliefs of the Sinhala people, with the cobra symbolizing water protection, and is a key historical landmark near the Hambantota-Suriyawewa road

Ridiyagama Tank

The Ridiyagama Tank (Wewa) in Sri Lanka's Hambantota District is a significant reservoir built under the Walawe Ganga Left Bank Scheme, constructed between 1923-1928, featuring a 1.5-mile bund and two spillways, serving as a crucial water source for irrigation and notably associated with the nearby Ridiyagama Farm and Safari Park, attracting visitors with its vast water body and surrounding attractions.

Liyangastota amuna

Liyangastota Amuna is a historic British-built diversion weir (anicut) on the Walawe Ganga River, constructed in 1889 to supply water to reservoirs like Ridiyagama and Kadawara via its left bank channel, with the right bank feeding Mamadola and Oluvila tanks. This 73m long, 5.8m high structure is a popular spot for nature lovers, photographers, and safe bathing during low water, featuring sluices and providing a glimpse into Sri Lanka's ancient and colonial irrigation engineering.

Udawalawe National Park

Udawalawe National Park in Sri Lanka is famous for its large, guaranteed elephant sightings, vast grasslands, and the Udawalawe Reservoir, offering a prime safari destination with diverse wildlife like buffalo, deer, crocodiles, and numerous bird species in a dry-zone landscape with scrub and forest. Established to protect displaced animals and the reservoir's catchment, it's a year-round park, best visited early morning for cooler temperatures and active animals.

Sinharaja Rain forest

The Sinharaja Forest Reserve is Sri Lanka's last significant lowland tropical rainforest, a UNESCO World Heritage Site known for its exceptional biodiversity, especially endemic species of flora and fauna, including rare birds, reptiles, and plants. Located in the southwest, it's a narrow, ridge-filled area crisscrossed by streams, featuring dense vegetation, high rainfall, and trekking trails leading to peaks like Moulawella and Sinhagala. .

Yala National Park

Yala National Park is a national park in Sri Lanka, bordering the Indian Ocean and covering 979 km2 (378 sq mi). It was designated as a wildlife sanctuary in 1900. It hosts diverse ecosystems ranging from moist monsoon forests to freshwater and marine wetlands, and is important for the conservation of Sri Lankan elephants, Sri Lankan leopards and 44 mammal species. It is one of the 70 Important Bird Areas in Sri Lanka harbouring 215 bird species including six endemic species of Sri Lanka. The area around Yala National Park has hosted several ancient civilizations. Two important Buddhist pilgrim sites, Sithulpahuwa and Magul Vihara, are situated within the park. The 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami caused severe damage on the Yala National Park, and 250 people died in its vicinity..

Ussangoda National Park

Ussangoda National Park in Sri Lanka is famous for its unique red, barren landscape, believed to be caused by a meteorite impact or high ferric oxide, creating a "Martian" feel with stunted grass, linked to Ramayana legends as King Ravana's landing site. This coastal park protects sea turtle breeding grounds, rare plants, and archaeological sites, offering stunning sea views where red earth meets the turquoise ocean, contrasting with lush surroundings.

Wavulpane Limestone Cave

Waulpane Limestone Cave, near Embilipitiya in Sri Lanka's Ratnapura District, is a massive ancient cave known as the "Cave of Bats," home to hundreds of thousands of bats, ancient fossils, and an impressive internal waterfall that flows year-round. Visitors experience a raw, adventurous trek through slippery paths, navigating a stream and bat guano to see stunning limestone formations (stalactites, stalagmites) and the unique internal waterfall, offering a glimpse into prehistoric geology. .
